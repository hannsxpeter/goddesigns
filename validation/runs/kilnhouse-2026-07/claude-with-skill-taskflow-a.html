Chapter 01

Start with a plan people can point at.

A TaskFlow project is a list of tasks anyone can read at a glance.

Each task gets one owner, one due date, and a status. That is the whole trick: the plan lives on the board instead of in someone's head, so "who has this?" stops being a meeting.

Chapter 02

One board, three honest views.

The same tasks, shown three ways. Change one, the other two follow.

Board

Columns for each status. Drag a card and the plan updates everywhere.

Timeline

Every due date on one strip, so collisions show up before they happen.

List

The whole project as a sortable list, for people who think in rows.

Chapter 03

Teach the board to do the boring parts.

Write a rule once and TaskFlow runs it every time. Rules read like sentences, because they should.

when status becomes "Done" -> move the card to Shipped

when a due date slips -> nudge the owner, not the whole channel

when a task sits idle 5 days -> flag it in the Friday digest

Chapter 05

Questions teams actually ask.

Can we import from the tool we use now?

+

Yes. TaskFlow reads CSV exports from most project tools. During import you map columns to owners, due dates, and statuses, and the board builds itself.

What happens when the trial ends?

+

Your boards go read-only. Nothing is deleted, and upgrading turns everything back on exactly as you left it.

Is there a limit on projects?

+

Starter caps you at 3 projects. Team and Business have no limit, and archived projects never count against you.

How does per-person pricing work?

+

You pay only for people who can edit. Viewers, guests, and clients who just need to see the plan are free on every tier.
